1-Developing Self-Advocacy:

  • Teach Label Reading: Help your child learn to read food labels and recognize dairy ingredients such as casein, whey, and lactose.
  • Role-Playing: Practice scenarios where your child needs to decline food politely or ask if a dish contains dairy.
  • Emergency Preparedness: Make sure your child understands what to do in case of accidental exposure, including how to use emergency medication if prescribed.

2-School and Extracurricular Activities:

  • 504 Plan: Consider setting up a 504 plan with your child’s school to ensure that their dietary needs are legally protected.
  • Safe Snacks for Group Activities: Provide a stash of dairy-free snacks for teachers to use during unexpected celebrations or class activities.
  • Extracurricular Activities: Communicate with coaches and leaders about your child’s allergy and provide safe snacks for sports events or group outings.

3-Psychological and Emotional Support:

  • Build Confidence: Encourage your child to talk openly about their allergy with friends and teachers.
  • Support Groups: Connect with other families dealing with dairy allergies through online forums or local support groups. This provides both you and your child with valuable advice and a sense of community.
  • Positive Reinforcement: Celebrate your child’s successes in managing their allergy and making safe choices.
